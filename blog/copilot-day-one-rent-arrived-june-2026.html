Day One: The Rent Already Arrived

June 1, 2026

Own vs Rent · Copilot Day 1

June 1, 2026. GitHub Copilot's token-based billing went live today. Within hours, the stories started rolling in.

The Numbers

A developer on Reddit posted their new bill: $29 per month became $750 per month. That's a 26x increase. On day one.

Another user shared a screenshot showing their costs jumping from $50 to $3,000 per month. That's 60x.

TechCrunch covered the backlash with the headline quote from one developer: "What a joke."

4.7 million paid subscribers woke up today to a fundamentally different product than the one they paid for yesterday. And the official community thread? 435 comments. 904 downvotes. 22 upvotes. That's a 41-to-1 rejection ratio.

The Safety Net Is Gone

Here's the part nobody expected: GitHub removed the safety net entirely.

Under the old system, when you ran out of premium requests, Copilot would fall back to a cheaper model. You could still keep working. Not anymore.

Under the new token-based billing, when your credits run out, you stop. Or you pay more. There is no fallback. The landlord didn't just raise the rent — he changed the locks and removed the spare key.

A $10/month Copilot Pro plan now includes 1,000 AI credits. A single agentic session running a frontier model across a multi-file codebase consumes 30 to 40 of those credits. Your monthly allotment can vanish in under two weeks. Then you're paying overage — or you're not coding.

The Same Word, 14 Days Apart

GitHub's Chief Product Officer Mario Rodriguez explained the change: "The current premium request model is no longer sustainable."

Fourteen days earlier, Boris Cherny, head of Claude Code at Anthropic, said third-party tools were "really hard to do sustainably."

Two vendors. Two weeks. The same word. This isn't a company problem. It's an industry model.

Rent goes up. Every time.
